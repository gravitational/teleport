Teleport's on-prem Enterprise deployment provides essential FedRAMP control coverage for the people, workloads, and AI agents operating inside your infrastructure. 
Teleport Enterprise is actively used inside FedRAMP-authorized services today, including environments audited at the High impact level.

## Why FedRAMP teams choose Teleport

Teleport solves authentication and authorization within your FedRAMP authorization boundary, giving you extensive coverage across the Access Control (AC) family 
along with controls in Audit and Accountability (AU), Identification and Authentication (IA), and System and Communications Protection (SC).
The platform governs who can reach your servers, clusters, databases, web applications, and desktops; what they are authorized to do once connected; and generates detailed audit logs and session recordings. 
  
The Teleport platform manages both human and non-human access to consistently answer the question "who did this, with what authority, and when?" anywhere in your environment.
  
* People — Users get [Single sign-on](../sso/sso.mdx) with phishing-resistant MFA, granting short-lived, role-scoped access to your internal services. No shared passwords, no long-lived SSH keys, no break-glass accounts, no standing accounts to revoke when someone leaves.
* Workloads — Internal applications, CI pipelines, and scripts are each issued a unique, short-lived, SPIFFE-compatible cryptographic identity through Teleport's [Machine & Workload Identity](../../machine-workload-identity/machine-workload-identity.mdx). Workloads authenticate to each other and to your databases, services, 
and cloud APIs over mutual TLS (mTLS) — guaranteeing cryptographic protection over the network while replacing static API keys and service-account credentials with auto-rotating identities under the same access and audit controls as your people.  Internal access between hosts gains consistent authentication and authorization that's centrally managed.
* AI — Autonomous agents and processes acting on behalf of users or workloads receive their own identity through Teleport's [Agentic Identity Framework](../../agentic-identity-framework.mdx).  Access to MCP servers, databases, and internal APIs is brokered, authorized, and audited by Teleport's identity-aware proxy.  Controlled, compliant access control for the new wave of AI tooling is now a solved problem.

In practice, a Teleport deployment in your FedRAMP environment standardizes access control across your ATO boundary that would otherwise be a collection of bespoke, ah-hoc services, password and secret management, logging and management functions - 
the sort of ad hoc implementations that are almost guaranteed to have security gaps leading to vulnerabilities and control findings.  
  
With Teleport, you and your 3PAO work through dozens of control implementations in one sitting - presenting evidence, demonstrating capabilities, and offering short, simple, accurate explanations.
Your team demonstrates consistent, thoughtful, compliant security while your assessors get all the information they need in one place (in particular they tend to love the UI, session-termination ban-hammer, and session recording system).  End result, everybody wins.


## FIPS 140-2 & FIPS 140-3 Support

Teleport Enterprise's built-in FIPS support enables a FIPS 140-validated cryptographic module on the platform to satisfy FISMA's cryptographic requirements under NIST SP 800-53 controls SC-13 (Cryptographic Protection) and IA-7 (Cryptographic Module Authentication).

- **FIPS 140-3**: Teleport releases from 17.7.3+ and 18.0.0+ use BoringCrypto tag `fips-20220613` ([CMVP certificate 4735](https://csrc.nist.gov/projects/cryptographic-module-validation-program/certificate/4735), FIPS 140-3)
- **FIPS 140-2**: Teleport releases prior to 17.7.3 use BoringCrypto tag `fips-20210429` ([CMVP certificate 4407](https://csrc.nist.gov/projects/cryptographic-module-validation-program/certificate/4407), FIPS 140-2) - sunsets September 21, 2026.

A detailed list of cryptographic algorithms used in FIPS mode is available below in [FIPS Cryptographic algorithms](#default-cryptographic-algorithms).
For more information on BoringCrypto's FIPS validation, see Google's [BoringCrypto FIPS Module documentation](https://boringssl.googlesource.com/boringssl/+/HEAD/crypto/fipsmodule/FIPS.md).


## Teleport for FedRAMP Deployment

A FedRAMP-compliant Teleport deployment has four pieces that must line up:

1. **Teleport Enterprise FIPS build.** Teleport FIPS builds are compiled against the FIPS 140-validated BoringCrypto module. This is what guarantees your platform's cryptographic operations are FIPS-validated.
2. **A FIPS-compatible cluster configuration.** The `teleport.yaml` (or Helm values) must disable local authentication and require an SSO connector. Clusters migrating from a non-FIPS deployment may also need to update their certificate authorities and signature algorithms.
3. **FIPS mode at cluster startup.** Your Teleport cluster must be started in FIPS mode, either via the `--fips` flag on a binary install or via the FIPS-aware container image. FIPS mode restricts the platform to FIPS-approved cipher suites, disables local authentication to mandate SSO, and routes audit storage to FIPS endpoints.
4. **FIPS Agents on every target host.** Each Linux host you want to access through Teleport runs the same `teleport` binary as the cluster, configured as a Teleport Agent with the appropriate service block (`ssh_service`, `db_service`, etc.) and started with `--fips`.

The sections below walk through each piece for both binary/systemd installs and Helm-based deployments.

If you're deploying with Helm, most of this is handled for you by the FIPS distroless image — it enables FIPS mode at startup automatically, so your remaining job is to point the chart at the right image and disable local authentication.

### 1. Teleport Enterprise FIPS build

For FedRAMP authorization, install the Teleport Enterprise FIPS build on your server hosts. The standard Enterprise binary is not FIPS-validated and will not satisfy SC-13 / IA-7. The FedRAMP/FIPS package also contains FIPS variants of `tsh`, `tctl`, and `tbot` — covered in step 4 below.

The FIPS build is available in two forms:
- **Tarball or package installs** for systemd-based hosts. Available from your [Teleport account](https://teleport.sh) — look for `Linux 64-bit (FedRAMP/FIPS)` — and installed using the standard [Teleport installation instructions](../../installation/installation.mdx).
- **Container image** for Docker, Kubernetes, and Helm deployments: `public.ecr.aws/gravitational/teleport-ent-fips-distroless`. Install using the standard [container installation instructions](../../installation/single-machine/docker.mdx).


### 2. A FIPS-compatible cluster configuration

A FIPS-compliant `teleport.yaml` (or Helm values file) must do two things at minimum: **disable local authentication** so all logins go through your SSO provider, and **require host-key checking** between Teleport components. 
Both are also set by `--fips` at runtime, but file-based configuration overrides defaults.  Putting them explicitly in the config ensures they aren't accidentally overwritten.

#### Auth Service configuration

Add the following settings to `/etc/teleport.yaml` on the Auth Service host:

```yaml
auth_service:
  authentication:
    # Disable local auth — all logins go through SSO.
    local_auth: false
    # Set to saml, oidc, or github depending on your IdP.
    type: <Var name="sso_type"/>

  # Required to start Teleport in FIPS mode.
  proxy_checks_host_keys: true
```

The SSO connector itself (SAML, OIDC, or GitHub) is configured separately. See the [Single sign-on](../sso/sso.mdx) docs for connector setup and be sure that your IdP has the necessary FedRAMP authorization.

#### Helm values

For Helm deployments using the FIPS distroless image, the equivalent FIPS-compatible values are:

```yaml
enterpriseImage: public.ecr.aws/gravitational/teleport-ent-fips-distroless
authentication:
  localAuth: false
```

The SSO connector itself is configured through chart values — see the [`teleport-cluster` chart reference](../../reference/helm-reference/teleport-cluster.mdx).



### 3. FIPS mode at cluster startup

Once the FIPS build is installed and configuration written, Teleport must be **started** in FIPS mode using the `--fips` flag for the FIPS-mode configuration constraints to fire. Without the `--fips` flag, the binary uses BoringCrypto for cryptographic operations but does not restrict the platform to FIPS-approved algorithms (a quirk of the BoringCrypto package — it's FIPS-validated, but still ships with non-FIPS libraries that Teleport must be told to avoid). The deployment is cryptographically validated but not operationally compliant.

#### Binary / systemd installs

Start `teleport` with the `--fips` flag:

```code
# Starting the cluster
$ teleport start --fips
```

For systemd-managed deployments, review the FIPS-specific service unit file from the [examples directory](https://github.com/gravitational/teleport/tree/branch/v(=teleport.major_version=)/examples/systemd/fips). The unit file already includes the `--fips` flag.

#### Container, Kubernetes, and Helm installs

The FIPS distroless image (`public.ecr.aws/gravitational/teleport-ent-fips-distroless`) starts Teleport in FIPS mode automatically — no entrypoint override or extra arguments needed.


### 4. FIPS Agents on every target host

Each Linux host you want to access through Teleport runs the same `teleport` binary as the cluster, configured as a [Teleport Agent](../../installation/agents/agents.mdx) instead of as the Auth or Proxy Service. Inside your authorization boundary, every Agent must use be the FIPS teleport build (same as the cluster).

Start the Agent with `--fips`, the same way as the cluster:

```code
# Starting the agent
$ teleport start --fips
```

Agents register with the cluster via a [join method](../../installation/agents/join-token.mdx) — static token, AWS IAM, Kubernetes ServiceAccount, GitHub, and others. The choice of join method does not affect FIPS posture; it only determines how the Agent authenticates itself to the cluster on startup.

The same FedRAMP/FIPS Linux package also contains FIPS variants of `tsh`, `tctl`, and `tbot`. 


## FAQ

### Teleport Enterprise Cloud 

Teleport Enterprise Cloud is **not FedRAMP authorized**.  Teleport Enterprise must be deployed within your ATO boundary and configured in FIPS mode as described above to meet FedRAMP requirements.

### AWS region requirement

Teleport Enterprise must be deployed in a US-based AWS region that holds an appropriate FedRAMP authorization. For Low and Moderate-impact authorizations, deploy in a region within [AWS's FedRAMP Moderate scope](https://aws.amazon.com/compliance/services-in-scope/FedRAMP/) (US East and US West). For High-impact authorizations, deploy in [AWS GovCloud (US)](https://aws.amazon.com/govcloud-us/).

### FedRAMP audit log

At the close of a connection (close of a \*srv.ServerContext), the total data transmitted and received is emitted to the Audit Log.

### mTLS

Teleport implements mTLS for all communications between user clients and Teleport servers, with several exceptions listed below.
Inside the ATO boundary, mTLS is used for communication between the Teleport proxy and internal hosts running all protocols.
Following successful authentication to the SSO Identity Provider, Teleport issues the authenticated user x.509 client certificates signed by its own internal x.509 CA. Target Teleport services and clients require valid x.509 certificates and mTLS for all target SSH, Kubernetes, database, and web application connections.

#### mTLS exceptions

- Teleport components provide read-only health check REST endpoints requiring only TLS.
- Teleport offers optional web UI for Windows Desktop and SSH. For web UI access, Teleport uses TLS + session cookie + bearer token with proxy. Teleport Proxy converts web sessions to mTLS to reach out to Teleport servers.
- Teleport offers optional SSH compatibility mode. In this mode Teleport dials target OpenSSH services using OpenSSH client certificates issued and signed by Teleport OpenSSH CA.
- The connection from the Teleport Proxy to Teleport desktop agent is mTLS, but the connection Teleport desktop agent to an RDP server is only TLS. The agent authenticates itself to the RDP server via a virtual PIV-compatible smartcard containing a user certificate issued by Teleport’s internal CA.



### FIPS

#### Verify you're running the FIPS build

Confirm Teleport is running the FIPS build by checking the version output for the BoringCrypto tag which indicates the library used for cryptographic operations.

```code
# Binary install
$ teleport version

# Container install
$ docker exec <container_name> teleport version

# Kubernetes or Helm install
$ kubectl exec <teleport_pod> -- teleport version
```

The output should end with `X:boringcrypto`. This indicates that Google's BoringCrypto module is being used for the platform's cryptographic operations.  
If it doesn't, you have a standard Enterprise build — Teleport will refuse to start with the `--fips` flag and your deployment will not satisfy SC-13 / IA-7.

You can also confirm the running teleport process was started in FIPS mode by inspecting its command line:

```code
$ ps aux | grep teleport
```
The `--fips` flag should appear in the output.


#### What `--fips` enforces

When Teleport starts in FIPS mode, it:

- Restricts TLS to versions 1.2 and 1.3 with FIPS-approved cipher suites only.
- Restricts SSH ciphers, key exchange, MAC, and public-key algorithms to the FIPS-approved set.
- Restricts certificate keys (including host keys when in recording proxy mode) to ECDSA on NIST P-256 or 2048-bit RSA.
- Removes non-FIPS algorithms from the binary entirely — for example, NaCl is replaced with AES-GCM.
- Disables local authentication, requiring an SSO connector for all logins.
- Routes audit storage (S3, DynamoDB) to FIPS endpoints when running on AWS.
- Forces session recording at the node.
- Always enables at-rest encryption for DynamoDB and S3.
- Refuses `--insecure` — Teleport will fail to start if both flags are set.
- Defaults to FIPS-compliant algorithms if no TLS or SSH cryptographic primitives are explicitly configured.


#### FIPS cryptographic algorithms

In FIPS mode, Teleport will use the following cryptographic algorithms by default.

Default FIPS ciphers for SSH:

- `aes128-gcm@openssh.com`
- `aes256-gcm@openssh.com`
- `aes128-ctr`
- `aes192-ctr`
- `aes256-ctr`

Default FIPS Key Exchange Algorithms (KEX) for SSH:

- `ecdh-sha2-nistp256`
- `ecdh-sha2-nistp384`

Default FIPS Message Authentication Codes (MAC) for SSH:

- `hmac-sha2-256-etm@openssh.com`
- `hmac-sha2-512-etm@openssh.com`
- `hmac-sha2-256`
- `hmac-sha2-512`

Default FIPS Public Key Authentication Algorithms for SSH:

- `ecdsa-sha2-nistp256`
- `ecdsa-sha2-nistp384`
- `rsa-sha2-256`
- `rsa-sha2-512`

Default FIPS cipher suites for TLS 1.2:

- `tls-ecdhe-ecdsa-with-aes-128-gcm-sha256`
- `tls-ecdhe-rsa-with-aes-128-gcm-sha256`
- `tls-ecdhe-ecdsa-with-aes-256-gcm-sha384`
- `tls-ecdhe-rsa-with-aes-256-gcm-sha384`

Default FIPS cipher suites for TLS 1.3:

- `tls-aes-128-gcm-sha256`
- `tls-aes-256-gcm-sha384`


#### Migrating from a non-FIPS deployment

Existing Teleport clusters created with v17+ default to Ed25519 certificate keys, which are **not** supported by the FIPS binary. Attempting to start the FIPS binary against an Ed25519-keyed cluster produces:

```
User Message: only RSA and ECDSA keys supported
```

To migrate:

1. Set the cluster's [signature algorithm suite](../../reference/deployment/signature-algorithms.mdx) to `fips-v1`.
2. Run a [CA rotation](../management/security/ca-rotation.mdx) for any CA that holds an Ed25519 key. Use `tctl status` to confirm the signature algorithms in use.
3. Once all CAs have rotated to FIPS-compatible algorithms, install the FIPS binary and proceed with the rest of this section.

#### Remote desktop access and TLS 1.2

Teleport uses Rust for RDP connections, and thus uses a fork of Cloudflare's [`BoringSSL bindings`](https://github.com/gravitational/boring)
library under the hood to enable Rust applications to use the built-in the Google FIPS-validated TLS cryptographic module.  The primary notable difference to the specifications listed above is that
TLS is restricted to TLS 1.2 only (1.3 is not supported).


#### FIPS Failure modes

- **`--fips` on a non-FIPS binary** — Teleport fails to start with: *"binary not compiled in FIPS140 mode, check that Enterprise FIPS release was downloaded from a Teleport account."*
- **FIPS binary without `--fips`** — Teleport starts and uses BoringCrypto for cryptographic operations, but does **not** enforce FIPS-mode policy. The deployment is cryptographically validated but not operationally compliant.
- **Non-FIPS cipher suites configured** — if `teleport.yaml` specifies TLS or SSH cryptographic primitives that are not FIPS 140 compliant, Teleport will fail to start.
- `arm64` FIPS builds do not support access to Windows desktops.

#### FIPS `tsh` constraints

A FIPS `tsh` enforces additional runtime constraints:
- **Refuses `--insecure`** — the flag is removed from the FIPS build entirely; it cannot be passed.
- **Validates server certificates against FIPS-approved algorithms** — RSA-2048, ECDSA P-256, or ECDSA P-384 only. Non-compliant keys produce a `FIPSError`.
- **Refuses to connect to non-FIPS clusters** — if the cluster presents non-FIPS material, `tsh` errors out with: *"tsh is running in FIPS mode, but the cluster is not FIPS-compliant. Use a non-FIPS tsh binary to connect to the cluster."*


## Tradeoffs of using the Teleport FIPS binary

Using Teleport's FIPS binary is designed to meet specific regulatory and compliance requirements (such as
FedRAMP in the US).  Enabling FIPS mode only impacts which cryptographic module and algorithms are available.

There are several tradeoffs when considering using Teleport FIPS builds in
cases where it is not strictly required.

### Restricted cipher suite selection

The FIPS build only permits ciphers, key exchange mechanisms, and signature
algorithms that are approved under the FIPS certification program. Due to the
rigorous and lengthy certification process, FIPS-approved algorithms often lag
behind the state of the art. As a result, the FIPS build lacks support for
newer algorithms with improved performance or forward-looking security
properties (e.g. ChaCha20-Poly1305, Ed25519).

### Reduced performance

Cryptographic operations may be up to 2× slower compared to the standard build.
This can impact throughput for high-volume clusters.

### Operational complexity

Running the FIPS build can introduce additional maintenance burden, such as
troubleshooting connection issues where peers expect different cipher support.

### No security benefit without compliance need

Outside of regulated environments, using the FIPS binary does not increase
Teleport's security posture. In fact, it may result in a net negative effect by
limiting use of modern cryptographic algorithms.
